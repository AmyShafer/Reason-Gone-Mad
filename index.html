Iconic and Universally Beloved

The Marx Brothers were an American family comedy act that was successful in vaudeville, on Broadway, and in motion pictures from 1905 to 1949. (Source: Wikipedia)

Vaudeville, Broadway, and Motion Picture Stars!

Five of the Marx Brothers' thirteen feature films were selected by the American Film Institute (AFI) as among the top 100 comedy films, with two of them, Duck Soup (1933) and A Night at the Opera (1935), in the top fifteen. (Source: Wikipedia)

Julius Henry "Groucho" Marx

Both Groucho's and Harpo's memoirs say that their now-famous on-stage personae were created by Al Shean. (Source: Wikipedia)

Adolph "Harpo" Marx was born in 1888.

By the 1920s, the Marx Brothers had become one of America's favorite theatrical acts, with their sharp and bizarre sense of humor. They satirized high society and human hypocrisy, and they became famous for their improvisational comedy in free-form scenarios. A famous early instance was when Harpo arranged to chase a fleeing chorus girl across the stage during the middle of a Groucho monologue to see if Groucho would be thrown off. However, to the audience's delight, Groucho merely reacted by commenting, "First time I ever saw a taxi hail a passenger". (Source: Wikipedia)

On January 16, 1977, the Marx Brothers were inducted into the Motion Picture Hall of Fame.

Among famous comedians who have cited them as influences on their style have been Mel Brooks, Carl Reiner, Monty Python, and Judd Apatow. (Source: Wikipedia)
